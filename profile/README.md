# Adamson Research Group

**Polymer and 2D-materials science at the University of Connecticut**

Led by Prof. Douglas H. Adamson in the Department of Chemistry and the Polymer Program (Institute of Materials Science) at UConn, the group works at two ends of materials synthesis: building polymers one monomer at a time with control over their architecture, and assembling 2D materials at interfaces into functional structures. Both lines aim at the same thing, materials whose properties follow from a structure known from the chemistry up: block copolymers, conductive composites, polymer foams, hydrogels, membranes, coatings, and drug-delivery carriers.

## High-vacuum anionic polymerization

We synthesize polymers by living anionic polymerization under high vacuum. Sealed glassware and rigorous exclusion of air and moisture keep the carbanion chain ends alive, so chains grow to a set length with narrow dispersity and terminate only when we choose. Adding monomers in sequence then builds defined architectures: diblock and triblock copolymers, grafts, stars, and brush structures, such as a poly(styrene-block-isoprene) backbone carrying poly(ethylene oxide) grafts. These well-defined polymers are the foundation for our work on self-assembly, block copolymer lithography, polymer brushes, and nanocarriers for drug delivery.

## Solvent Interface Trapping Method (SITM)

At the other end of our work, unmodified graphite, along with other 2D materials such as hexagonal boron nitride, acts as a surfactant at an oil/water interface, spreading and exfoliating into single sheets. No oxidation, no chemical functionalization, no added surfactant. A single step exfoliates pristine sheets and traps them in a percolating network, which is the starting point for much of our work on graphene foams, polyHIPEs, hydrogels, and conductive composites.

## Research areas

- Living anionic polymerization and precise block copolymer architectures
- Self-assembly, block copolymer lithography, and polymer brushes
- Nanocarriers and drug delivery
- Graphene and 2D materials: exfoliation, dispersion, and oxide chemistry
- Graphene-stabilized emulsions, polymer foams (polyHIPE), and hydrogels
- Conductive and flame-resistant composites, membranes, sensing, and energy storage
